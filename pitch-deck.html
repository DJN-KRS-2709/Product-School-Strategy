The Thesis

Content ages faster than courses can be rewritten.

Product School's nine certifications share a single structural problem. The solution is not to rewrite faster — it is to change the unit of content from course to concept.

The Diagnosis

Five structural problems

  • 1
    Framework drift. The same concept is taught differently across courses. Agent Spectrum has two incompatible definitions. No canonical source of truth.
  • 2
    Content staleness. AI capabilities change weekly. No mechanism propagates an update across the other eight certifications that reference the same concept.
  • 3
    Feedback silos. Three Vibe Coding cohorts drove a full redesign — but none of that signal reached AI Agents or AI for PMs, where topics overlap.
  • 4
    Duplication of effort. The Vibe Coding redesign found 55–60% content reuse — but discovery took manual archaeology. Every new course repeats this.
  • 5
    Ad-hoc external signal. Lenny insights, LinkedIn frameworks, podcast transcripts — curated by individuals, benefiting exactly one course.
Root Cause

The unit of content management is the course, when it should be the concept.

Just as the old AI Prototyping course "tried to teach a tool" when it should have been "teaching a process," our content operations try to maintain courses when they should be maintaining knowledge.

Q1 — The Bet
"You can't predict which AI feature wins. Stop pretending you can."
— AI Product Strategy, M1

Build a course-agnostic content library.

Every teachable concept, framework, case study, and insight is stored as an independent, reusable content atom. Courses consume atoms. Feedback enriches atoms. The library is the product; courses are the distribution channels.

Q1 — The Bet

The content atom

The smallest unit of teachable knowledge that can exist independently of any specific course.

◆

Concept

A named idea with a canonical definition, context, and version history. E.g. "Confidence UX" — three tiers making probabilistic output legible.

▲

Framework

A reusable mental model with structure and application guidance. E.g. "5 AI Value Archetypes" — each with margin profile and governance burden.

●

Evidence

A case study, data point, or real-world example with source attribution and recency. E.g. "Klarna Automator overcorrection" — 3-act case study.

⚡

Signal

A raw external input — podcast excerpt, LinkedIn post, research finding — with extracted insight. E.g. Gokul Rajaram on brand moats, 20VC March 2026.

Q2 — The Moat
"Your data is not your moat. Your workflow is."
— AI Product Strategy, M2

Three compounding loops

A shared content library is table-stakes. The moat is the compounding loops that make it more valuable with every interaction.

Loop 1: Cohort Feedback → Atom Refinement

Every cohort generates signal: surveys, in-class questions, instructor adaptations. Signal routes to atoms. When 3 cohorts flag a concept, it gets enriched — and every referencing course benefits.

Loop 2: External Signal → New Atoms

Lenny publishes on AI pricing. Gokul defines 8 Moats. A LinkedIn post goes viral. Systematic ingestion turns individual research into institutional knowledge across all 9 courses.

Loop 3: Cross-Course Citation → Authority

Atoms referenced by 5+ courses are foundationally important. Citation count becomes a natural priority signal for maintenance, verification, and depth of treatment.

Q5 — The Guardrails
"Your governance framework is a sales asset, not a compliance burden."
— AI Product Strategy, M5

Content governance model

Who creates atoms?

Anyone

Course authors, instructors, content team, CPTO. Low barrier ensures the library captures signal from everywhere.

Who promotes to Validated?

Content leads

Requires source verification, peer review, and a decision on which courses should reference the atom.

Who deprecates?

CPTO + course leads jointly

Deprecated atoms are archived, not deleted — retaining full history and rationale.
